Candlestick Patterns

A single candle tells the entire story of the battle between buyers and sellers in one visual. Body, wicks, size, position — everything carries meaning. The most common mistake: reading candles in isolation without context.

⚡ Critical Insight

A Doji is NOT a signal — it is a question.

A Doji in the middle of a range: means nothing. The same Doji after an uptrend, at strong resistance, with high volume, in the NY Kill Zone: that is a setup. Context is everything.

🕯 Candlestick Explorer — 51 Patterns (15 Bullish · 16 Bearish · 5 Neutral)
⚠️

Most common mistake: Patterns ohne Kontext handeln. A hammer only works: (1) after a clear downtrend, (2) at a relevant support/demand zone, (3) ideally with elevated volume, (4) in the correct time window. Remove any one factor — probability drops sharply.

Chart Formations

Read This Before Using the Explorer

A chart formation is never a signal on its own. It is a hypothesis that requires context to become a trade.

Every pattern below carries a win rate and bias — but those statistics only hold when the pattern appears in the right conditions. A head and shoulders at random means nothing. The same pattern at a Daily resistance zone, after a BSL sweep, in the London Kill Zone, with declining volume on the right shoulder — that is an 83% win rate setup. The formation is the final confirmation, not the reason to trade.

📐
1. Market Structure First
Is the HTF trend bullish or bearish? Only trade formations that align with the higher timeframe bias. A bullish flag in a bearish daily trend is a short setup, not a long one.
💧
2. Support & Resistance Context
Is the pattern forming at a significant price zone? Key S/R, Order Blocks and FVGs act as the structural foundation. A pattern in empty space has no edge. A pattern at a major level does.
⚡
3. Momentum & Regime
Is the market trending or ranging? Continuation patterns (flags, pennants) need a trending regime. Reversal patterns (H&S, double tops) need a regime that is exhausting. ADX tells you which one you are in.
🧠
4. Market Psychology
Every pattern is a visual record of crowd behaviour. A double top shows that buyers tried twice and failed — sellers absorbed every attempt. Read the emotion behind the candles, not just the shape.

How to learn formations: Study them one category at a time. Master the 7 bullish reversals before touching bearish ones. For each pattern, ask: what is the crowd doing here, why does it tend to fail, and what confirms it is real rather than a fake-out. Click any formation below to see full detail — then find it on a live chart before moving on.

Technical Indicators

Indicators describe the past in transformed form. They do not predict the future. Most retail traders overload their charts and see contradictory signals. Less is always more.

⚠️

Indicator Illusion: No indicator works reliably in isolation. RSI-Oversold in a strong downtrend can remain oversold for weeks. MACD crossovers in ranging markets generate 80% false signals. Indicators are confirmation tools, not entry signals.

INDICATOR LIBRARY

Moving Average (MA)TREND

Smooths price data over a defined period. SMA (simple), EMA (exponential, reacts faster to recent price). Popular periods: 20, 50, 100, 200.

📍 Define trend bias. Price above 200 EMA = bullish bias. Golden Cross / Death Cross as broad trend filters.
RSI (Relative Strength Index)MOMENTUM

Measures the speed and magnitude of price movements. Scale 0–100. Above 70 = Overbought, below 30 = Oversold. In strong trends, RSI can remain extreme for extended periods.

📍 Look for divergences: price makes Higher High, RSI makes Lower High → Bearish Divergence. Far more reliable than simple level-based trades.
MACDMOMENTUM

Moving Average Convergence Divergence. Shows the relationship between two EMAs (default: 12/26/9). Histogram shows momentum strength — bars shrinking = momentum fading. Crossovers are significantly lagging and best used as confirmation, not triggers.

📍 Best use: divergence analysis (price makes new high, MACD histogram does not = bearish divergence). Use for trend confirmation after a breakout, not as an entry trigger.
Bollinger BandsVOLATILITY

2 standard deviations around an SMA (default: 20). Shows relative volatility — wider bands mean higher volatility. Squeeze (narrow bands) = low volatility building before an imminent breakout.

📍 Volatility context and squeeze detection. In combination mit Volumen und Candlesticks stark.
ATR (Average True Range)VOLATILITY

Average true price range over a defined period. Essential for stop-loss sizing: Stop = Entry ± 1.5–2× ATR.

📍 Stop-Loss und Position-Sizing. Ein ATR-basierter Stop ist objektiv und marktbezogen — verhindert zu enge Stops.
Volume ProfileVOLUME

Shows where the most volume was traded. POC (Point of Control) = level with the highest volume. Value Area = 70% of total volume.

📍 S/R-Identifikation based on real trading activity. Significantly more precise than horizontal lines alone.
Ichimoku CloudTREND

Japanese system with 5 lines. The Cloud (Kumo) creates dynamic S/R zones. Price above the cloud = bullish. Self-contained and self-referential system.

📍 Best used on higher timeframes (Daily, Weekly) as trend context. Requires significant study time for beginners.
Stochastic OscillatorOSCILLATOR

Compares the closing price to the trading range over a period. Similar to RSI but faster-reacting. Most useful for mean-reversion strategies in ranging markets.

📍 Ranging markets for overbought/oversold entries. Combine with S/R for more precise entries.

XABCD Harmonic Patterns

Harmonic patterns use Fibonacci ratios to identify precise reversal zones (PRZ). Complex but highly precise when correctly identified.

🦋
Butterfly Pattern — High-Precision Reversal Zone
▼
5-point pattern (X-A-B-C-D). Point D lies beyond point X.
  • AB: retracement of XA to 78.6%
  • BC: retracement of AB to 38.2–88.6%
  • CD: extension of 127–161.8% of XA
  • PRZ around point D → reversal is expected here
  • Always wait for a confirmation candle — no blind entry
🦀
Gartley Pattern — The Oldest Harmonic Pattern
▼
H.M. Gartley (1935). Point D at 78.6% of XA.
  • AB: retracement to 61.8%
  • BC: retracement to 38.2–88.6%
  • CD: retracement to 78.6% of XA
  • Bullish Gartley: buy zone around D. Very reliable when all ratios match within ±10% tolerance
🦇
Bat, Crab & Shark
▼
  • Bat: D at 88.6% of XA. Tight stop possible → excellent R:R. Considered the "most precise harmonic pattern."
  • Crab: D at 161.8% of XA — beyond X. When it works: enormous moves. High failure rate.
  • Shark: 4 points (O-X-A-B-C). B at 88.6–113% of OX. Continuation-oriented.
  • Learn Gartley and Bat first — the others build on them.

Momentum, Mean Reversion & Indicators Deep Dive

Two fundamentally opposite approaches to reading markets — and a set of precision tools for each. Before picking an indicator, you must identify the regime. The same indicator that works brilliantly in a trend destroys accounts in a range.

⚡ The Core Principle

Markets trend roughly 30% of the time. The rest is ranging or consolidation.

Momentum strategies lose in ranges. Mean-reversion strategies lose in trends. The most common trader mistake: applying a trend-following indicator in a range, or a mean-reversion indicator in a trend. Regime identification comes first — always.

Momentum Indicators — Deep Dive

📈
RSI — Relative Strength Index (The Most Misused Tool)
▼

RSI measures the speed and magnitude of recent price changes on a 0–100 scale. Standard settings: 14 periods. Overbought: above 70. Oversold: below 30.

The critical misuse: in a strong downtrend, RSI can stay below 30 for weeks. "Oversold" ≠ "buy signal." Oversold means sellers are winning hard — not that they're done.

  • Correct use 1 — Divergence: RSI makes lower high while price makes higher high → bearish divergence → momentum fading. This is RSI's strongest signal.
  • Correct use 2 — Trend confirmation: RSI staying above 50 in an uptrend confirms trend health. Drop below 50 = first warning.
  • Correct use 3 — Hidden divergence: RSI makes higher low while price makes lower low (in uptrend) → bullish hidden divergence → trend continuation signal.
  • Wrong use: Buying RSI 28 in a downtrend. Selling RSI 72 in a strong uptrend. Level alone = low edge.
⚡
MACD — Momentum Shift & Histogram Analysis
▼

MACD = EMA12 − EMA26. Signal line = EMA9 of MACD. Histogram = MACD − Signal. Default settings: 12/26/9.

  • Histogram contraction: bars getting smaller → momentum slowing → potential reversal setup forming. This is more useful than the crossover itself.
  • Zero line crossover: MACD crossing above/below zero = medium-term momentum shift. Stronger than signal line crossover.
  • Signal line crossover: most traders use this but it lags significantly. Best used as secondary confirmation, not primary trigger.
  • Divergence (best use): price makes new high, MACD histogram makes lower high → bearish divergence. Price makes new low, histogram makes higher low → bullish.
  • In ranging markets: MACD generates constant false crossovers. Filter with ADX — only use MACD when ADX > 20.
🎯
ADX — Quantifying Trend Strength Objectively
▼

ADX measures trend strength regardless of direction (0–100). It does NOT tell you which direction — only how strong the trend is. This makes it the best regime-identification tool.

  • ADX < 20: weak or no trend — use mean reversion strategies
  • ADX 20–25: trend developing — wait for confirmation before momentum entries
  • ADX > 25: strong trend confirmed — momentum strategies have edge
  • ADX > 40: very strong trend — continuation likely, but exhaustion approaching
  • ADX declining from high: trend losing strength — consider taking profits
  • +DI above −DI + ADX > 25: strong uptrend confirmed. −DI above +DI + ADX > 25: strong downtrend.
📊
Bollinger Bands — Volatility & Mean Reversion Tool
▼

Middle band: SMA20. Upper/lower bands: ±2 standard deviations. 95% of all prices statistically fall within the bands.

  • Bollinger Squeeze: bands narrow to historically tight levels → volatility compression → imminent expansion. One of the most useful setups across all markets. Direction unclear until breakout occurs.
  • Band riding: in a strong trend, price can hug the upper (uptrend) or lower (downtrend) band for extended periods. This is NOT overbought — it is a trend-following signal.
  • Mean reversion use: price touching the outer band in a range → potential reversion to middle band (SMA20). Only valid when ADX < 20 and price is clearly ranging.
  • %B indicator: measures where price is within the bands. Above 1.0 = above upper band. Below 0 = below lower band. Useful for quantifying extremes.
  • Width indicator: tracks band separation — used to identify squeezes before breakouts.
🌀
Stochastic Oscillator — Precision Mean Reversion
▼

Stochastic compares closing price to the high-low range over a period. %K (fast) and %D (slow signal line). Standard: 14,3,3. Range: 0–100.

  • Cross within overbought/oversold: %K crossing below %D while above 80 = sell signal. %K crossing above %D while below 20 = buy signal. Most reliable when the cross happens within the extreme zone.
  • Divergence: same principle as RSI — stochastic failing to reach new extreme while price does = momentum fading.
  • Best use case: mean reversion in clearly ranging markets. At support/resistance levels in a range. Combine with RSI for double confirmation.
  • Worst use case: trending markets. Stochastic stays oversold/overbought in strong trends for extended periods, generating constant false signals.
🔗
Combining Indicators — The Confluence Rule
▼

Never use a single indicator. Combine one from each category:

  • Trend direction: EMA position (price above/below EMA200) + structure (HH/HL)
  • Regime identification: ADX (above/below 25)
  • Momentum confirmation: RSI or MACD histogram direction
  • Entry precision: Fibonacci retracement level + candlestick pattern
  • Example setup: Daily EMA200 = bullish → ADX 28 (strong trend) → price pulls back to 61.8% Fib + rising EMA50 → RSI at 48 (not oversold, healthy) → Bullish engulfing forms → enter long
  • Rule: 3 or more confluent signals = trade. 1–2 signals = pass. More signals from different categories = higher statistical edge.

Market Structure, S/R & Market Behaviour

This is the chapter that makes everything else work. Patterns, indicators and Fibonacci levels all fail without this foundation. Before reading a single candle or formation, you must be able to answer one question: what is the market doing right now, and why?

The Master Principle

Price is not random. It moves from liquidity to liquidity, through zones of supply and demand, in the direction of the dominant timeframe bias.

Understanding this sentence completely — and being able to identify each element on a live chart — is the difference between a trader who guesses and a trader who reads the market. Every concept below builds toward this ability.

Part 1 — Market Structure: The Skeleton of Price

Market structure is the sequence of swing highs and swing lows on your chart. It is the single most objective piece of information available. Before any indicator, before any pattern — determine the structure.

📈
Bullish Structure — Higher Highs, Higher Lows
▼

Bullish structure = each new swing high exceeds the previous, and each new swing low is also higher than the previous. This is the most objective definition of an uptrend. While this pattern holds, the bias is bullish — period.

  • Trading rule: only seek long setups. Every short against a bullish structure is a counter-trend trade with lower probability.
  • The key higher low: in a bullish trend, the most recent swing low is the most critical level. As long as it holds, the trend is intact.
  • Break of Structure (BOS) bullish: price breaks above the last swing high → confirmation the uptrend continues → look to buy the next pullback.
  • Break of Structure (BOS) bearish warning: price breaks below the last swing low → first structural warning → reduce long exposure, wait for confirmation.
  • Change of Character (CHoCH): a sharp, impulsive break of the last swing low (not a slow grind) → high probability the trend is reversing → shift bias to bearish and look for shorts on the next rally.
  • On lower timeframes: identify CHoCH on the 15M or 1H as a precision entry trigger within the direction of the Daily bias.
📉
Bearish Structure — Lower Highs, Lower Lows
▼

Bearish structure = each new swing high is lower than the previous, and each new swing low is also lower. Objective definition of a downtrend. Bias is bearish while this holds.

  • Trading rule: only seek short setups. Every long against bearish structure is counter-trend with lower probability.
  • The key lower high: the most recent swing high is the critical resistance. As long as rallies fail below the last lower high, the downtrend is intact.
  • BOS bearish: price breaks below last swing low → downtrend confirmed → sell rallies to the next lower high.
  • Bullish CHoCH: a sharp impulsive break above the last lower high → possible trend reversal → shift to neutral/bullish, wait for confirmation structure.
  • Multi-leg structure: in a strong downtrend, expect 3–5 bearish impulse legs with corrective bounces (lower highs). Each bounce that fails at a previous support (now resistance) confirms the trend.
↔️
Ranging Structure — The Neutral Zone
▼

When neither higher highs/lows nor lower highs/lows are cleanly forming, the market is ranging — consolidating between two roughly horizontal levels. This is the market's most common state (~70% of the time).

  • Identification: ADX below 20, price bouncing between defined highs and lows without making new extremes.
  • Strategy in ranges: mean reversion. Buy at range support, sell at range resistance. Do NOT use momentum strategies in ranges — they generate constant false signals.
  • Institutional behaviour in ranges: ranges are accumulation or distribution phases. High volume with little price movement = institutions building positions. Watch for the breakout direction.
  • False breaks: the most dangerous aspect of ranges. Price briefly breaks above resistance (triggering breakout buyers) then reverses — a stop hunt. Wait for a second close outside the range before treating a break as genuine.
  • Range breakout trade: wait for a genuine close outside the range + volume confirmation + retest of the broken level + continuation candle. This is significantly more reliable than trading the initial break.

Part 2 — Support & Resistance: Where Price Reacts

Support and resistance are the price zones where buying and selling pressure have historically caused price to react. They are not lines — they are zones. And in SMC/ICT methodology, they are more specifically understood as liquidity pools.

🏗️
How S/R Zones Form — The Psychology Behind the Level
▼

Every significant support or resistance level exists because a large number of traders made decisions at that price. Understanding why a level formed tells you how strong it is and how price will behave when it returns.

  • Swing highs: where sellers overwhelmed buyers — many long positions were stopped out, many shorts were entered. Both groups now watch this level with orders attached.
  • Swing lows: where buyers overwhelmed sellers — shorts were stopped, longs entered. Same effect in reverse.
  • Round numbers (1.2000, 3000, 50000): psychological anchors. Banks and algorithms cluster limit orders at round numbers — the data confirms this across every market. Always check if a S/R coincides with a round number.
  • Previous structure (weekly/monthly levels): the most significant S/R zones are the ones that held on the weekly or monthly chart. These are institutional levels — not retail technical analysis. The more time that has passed since a level was created, the more significant the reaction when it is revisited.
  • High-volume nodes: from Volume Profile analysis — the price levels where the most trading has historically occurred. These act as magnets and strong S/R zones simultaneously.
🔄
Role Reversal — The Most Reliable S/R Concept
▼

When price breaks through a resistance level and the break is genuine, that resistance becomes the new support. This role reversal is one of the most statistically reliable and most commonly observed phenomena in all of technical analysis.

  • Why it works: when resistance breaks, the traders who were short there now have losing positions. When price returns to the breakout level, they close their shorts (buying), and new buyers enter. This combined buying pressure turns the old resistance into support.
  • Entry model: (1) price breaks above resistance with a strong close, (2) price pulls back to the breakout level, (3) a rejection candle forms at the level, (4) enter long with stop below the zone. This is one of the highest-probability entries in all price action trading.
  • ICT/SMC context: the pullback to a broken resistance is often a liquidity delivery event — institutions are selling into the retail longs who bought the breakout, then buying back cheaper as the level holds.
  • Confirmation filter: the retest should hold on a closing basis. A wick that touches the old resistance but the candle closes above it = very strong signal. A close below the old resistance = the break was false.
🎯
S/R as Liquidity Zones — The SMC Reframe
▼

In Smart Money Concepts, support and resistance levels are not barriers — they are liquidity pools. This reframe changes how you trade them entirely.

  • Support is SSL: stop-losses from long positions cluster below support. When institutions want to buy, they drive price into the SSL zone, triggering the stops (creating sell orders), and buy into that selling pressure. Then price rises.
  • Resistance is BSL: stop-losses from short positions cluster above resistance. Institutions wanting to sell drive price above the BSL, triggering short stops (creating buy orders), and sell into that buying. Then price falls.
  • Practical implication: when price approaches a clear support level, expect a brief false break below before the real reversal. Do not enter longs exactly at support — wait for the false break and the reversal candle. Your entry is better and your stop is tighter.
  • Equal highs and lows: two swing highs at the same price = a double BSL cluster. Price will almost certainly sweep both before reversing. Same for equal lows (double SSL). These are the highest-probability stop-hunt targets in any chart.

Part 3 — Market Psychology: Reading What the Crowd is Doing

Every candle and every pattern is a visual record of human decisions made under pressure. The trader who understands crowd psychology reads the market differently — they see the fear, the greed and the traps that are invisible to the pattern-matcher.

😱
Fear and Greed Cycles — The Engine of Every Pattern
▼

Every market pattern — every single one — is driven by the same two emotions at different stages of the same cycle:

  • Accumulation (Smart Money buys quietly): retail sees a boring, directionless market and walks away. Volume is elevated but price barely moves. This is institutionally manufactured boredom — the range that precedes the move.
  • Markup (the trend begins): retail gets interested as price rises. FOMO builds. Late-cycle retail buyers enter near the top. Smart money is already fully positioned and beginning to distribute.
  • Distribution (Smart Money sells to retail): price plateaus at the top. Retail reads the consolidation as bullish. Volume is high but price stops advancing — sellers are absorbing every buyer. Media coverage peaks here.
  • Markdown (the fall): price collapses. Retail holds, averages down, then panic-sells at the bottom — precisely when Smart Money begins accumulating again.
  • The pattern implication: a head and shoulders forms because this cycle happens at the top of a move. The left shoulder = first retail enthusiasm. The head = FOMO peak. The right shoulder = distribution trap. The neckline break = markdown begins. Understanding the psychology makes the pattern inevitable, not just recognisable.
🪤
Bull Traps & Bear Traps — Designed Misdirection
▼

The most common reason formations fail is that the initial signal is a trap. Understanding traps is as important as understanding the patterns themselves.

  • Bull trap: price breaks above a resistance level, triggering buying from breakout traders, then immediately reverses. The breakout buyers are now trapped long in a falling market. Their eventual stop-out selling accelerates the downtrend.
  • Bear trap: price breaks below a support level, triggering short sellers, then reverses sharply upward. The shorts are trapped and their stop-losses (buy orders) fuel the rally.
  • How to avoid: (1) require a candle close on the other side of the level, not just a wick, (2) check volume — genuine breakouts have expanding volume, traps often have declining or spike-then-collapse volume, (3) wait for a retest — a genuine break will hold the broken level on the retest, a trap will not.
  • ICT framing: the trap IS the trade. When you see a false break of an obvious level — a brief spike through resistance or support that immediately reverses — that spike is the stop hunt. Enter in the reversal direction after the rejection candle.
  • Equal highs double top trap: retail sees a "double top" and shorts it. Price briefly breaks above both highs (the BSL sweep), stops them out, then drops hard. The retail short-seller who waited for the real break after the sweep makes the trade; the one who shorted the double top is stopped out of a profitable setup.
🌊
Momentum vs. Exhaustion — Reading the Speed of Price
▼

The speed, size and character of candles tells you whether the market has fuel or is running out of it. This is one of the most advanced skills in reading price action.

  • Expanding candles in trend direction: each candle is larger than the last → trend is accelerating → momentum is building → do not fight it. Pullbacks will be shallow.
  • Shrinking candles in trend direction: each candle is smaller than the last → trend is decelerating → energy is fading → a reversal or deep correction is approaching. This is the visual equivalent of MACD histogram contraction.
  • Large candles against the trend: a single large candle that reverses a prior trend's move in one session → this is institutional order flow → take it seriously. The market is showing you where Smart Money is positioned.
  • Climax candles: an extremely large candle at the end of a long trend (often with very high volume) → likely a blowoff top or capitulation bottom. The last buyers/sellers in a desperate rush → immediate reversal follows in the majority of cases.
  • The pause candle: a very small-bodied candle (inside bar, doji, spinning top) after a strong move → energy is compressing → the next candle will likely determine the near-term direction. This is where confirmation matters most.
🔗
Multi-Timeframe Analysis — Aligning All Signals
▼

The same price level means completely different things on different timeframes. A trader who only reads one timeframe is like a navigator looking at a city map while ignoring the country map.

  • The hierarchy: Monthly → Weekly → Daily → 4H → 1H → 15M → 5M. Higher timeframes dominate. A bullish setup on the 5M inside a bearish daily structure has significantly reduced probability.
  • HTF (Daily/Weekly): defines the master bias and the most significant S/R levels. These levels are the ones institutions actually react to. Mark them first, before touching a lower timeframe.
  • MTF (4H/1H): identifies the setup and the entry zone. The pattern or formation you are trading should be visible here. The structure should align with the daily bias.
  • LTF (15M/5M): provides the precise entry trigger. A bullish engulfing on the 5M inside a 4H demand zone that aligns with a daily uptrend is a three-timeframe confluent entry. This is the professional standard.
  • The drill-down process: (1) Daily — what is the overall bias and where are the major levels? (2) 4H — is price at a relevant zone within that daily context? (3) 15M/5M — is there a trigger candle confirming entry? If all three align → enter. If they conflict → wait.
  • Critical rule: never trade a pattern that contradicts the daily structure. A beautiful double bottom on the 15M inside a daily downtrend is a lower-probability setup. The same pattern as a correction within a daily uptrend is high probability.
