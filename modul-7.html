What Is Edge?

Edge is the statistical advantage your strategy produces over a large sample of trades. It is not about being right on any single trade. It is about having a positive expected value over time — so that the more trades you take, the more money you make.

The Professional Definition

A casino with a 2% edge over millions of bets becomes a billion-dollar business. You need the same precision.

A casino does not know if the next bet will win or lose. Neither do you. But if your strategy has positive expected value — even +0.15R per trade — over 500 trades that is +75R profit. Professional trading is not about prediction. It is about execution of a positive EV process.

🔬
How to Know If You Have an Edge
▼
  • Minimum sample size: 100 trades is the absolute minimum. 200–300 is statistically meaningful. Fewer than 100 = no conclusion possible about your strategy's edge.
  • What to measure: win rate, average winning trade size (in R), average losing trade size (in R), maximum consecutive losses, maximum drawdown %.
  • Positive edge threshold: EV = (win rate × avg win R) − (loss rate × avg loss R) > 0. If this is positive and consistent over 200+ trades, you have an edge.
  • Edge decay: markets change. An edge that worked perfectly for 2 years may weaken. Track your EV quarterly — if it drops toward zero, review and adapt.
  • The journal is your edge instrument: without a trade journal, edge measurement is impossible. This is why Module 4 exists before Module 7.
📉
Losing Without Losing Your Edge
▼

A 10-trade losing streak is not evidence of a broken strategy. It is statistically expected even in a strategy with a 60% win rate.

  • Probability of N consecutive losses: at 40% loss rate: 10 losses in a row = 0.4^10 = 0.01% probability. Rare but not impossible — and not a signal to stop trading.
  • The correct response to a losing streak: check that you are following your rules. If yes → trust the process. If no → stop, fix the rule-following problem, then continue.
  • Psychological edge leakage: the silent killer. You have edge on paper but lose in practice because of FOMO, revenge trading and premature exits. Edge without discipline = theoretical edge only.
  • The 3-strike rule: 3 consecutive losses in a session → mandatory break. 5 consecutive losses in a week → review all 5 trades before continuing. 10 consecutive losses → full strategy review.

The Most Common Mistake

A 70% win rate that wins 0.8R and loses 1.4R has negative EV (−0.14R per trade). This is the most common retail profile — high win rate, small winners, large losers. The feeling of winning 70% of the time masks the slow account destruction. Win rate means nothing without the R:R attached to it.

The Fixed Fractional Method — The Professional Standard
▼

Risk a fixed percentage of your current account balance on each trade. The percentage never changes. As the account grows, the dollar risk grows proportionally. As the account shrinks, the risk shrinks automatically.

  • Beginners: 0.5–1% per trade. At 1%, you need 100 consecutive total losses to lose everything. Virtually impossible with a positive EV strategy.
  • Intermediate: 1–2% per trade. Standard institutional allocation is typically 1–1.5%.
  • Maximum professional limit: 2% per trade. Going above 2% introduces variance risk that even strong edges cannot reliably overcome.
  • Never use fixed dollar risk: as your account grows, fixed dollar risk becomes too small as a percentage. As it shrinks, it becomes too large — accelerating losses.
  • Formula: Position Size = (Account × Risk%) ÷ (Stop Distance in $ per unit)
🎰
Kelly Criterion — Optimal Position Sizing Theory
▼

The Kelly Criterion calculates the theoretically optimal position size to maximise geometric growth rate. Formula: f* = (EV) / (variance), simplified as: f* = W − (1−W)/R where W = win rate and R = average win/average loss ratio.

  • Example: 45% win rate, 2.5R average win / 1R average loss. Kelly = 0.45 − (0.55/2.5) = 0.45 − 0.22 = 0.23 = 23% of account per trade.
  • Why 23% is too much: full Kelly is mathematically optimal for long-run growth but produces enormous short-term drawdowns. The psychological and practical risks make it unsuitable.
  • Half-Kelly: many professionals use 25–50% of the Kelly number. In the example above: 0.5 × 23% = 11.5%. Still high — shows even Half-Kelly is above retail risk tolerances.
  • Practical use: use Kelly to establish a theoretical ceiling (this is the maximum size that makes mathematical sense). Then risk 10–15% of Kelly for practical, psychologically manageable sizing.
📊
Scaling In and Scaling Out — Advanced Execution
▼
  • Scaling in: adding to a position as it moves in your favour. Only valid when the original thesis is confirmed. Never add to a losing position.
  • Pyramiding: systematic scaling in. Entry 1: 50% of planned size. Confirmation → Entry 2: 30%. Further confirmation → Entry 3: 20%. Stops trail up as entries are added.
  • Scaling out: closing portions of a position at different targets. Reduces risk, locks profits, allows remainder to run for large winners.
  • Standard split: close 50% at 1:1 R:R (moves stop to break-even), close remaining 50% at 1:3 or trail to capture the full move.
  • Partial close benefit: psychologically much easier to hold a runner when you have already locked profit on 50%. Prevents premature full exit on normal retracements.

Setting Targets — Logical vs. Optimistic
▼
  • Logical target: the next significant liquidity level in the direction of your trade (next swing high for longs, next swing low for shorts). Price is algorithmically drawn to these levels.
  • The first liquidity target: the closest BSL or SSL. This is the minimum target — where at least partial profit should be taken.
  • Extended target: the Fibonacci extension of the current move (127.2% or 161.8%). Use only when the first target provides 1:1 R:R minimum.
  • Do NOT set targets in empty air: "I want 3R" is not a target. 3R must coincide with a real market structure level. If the nearest logical target only gives you 1.2R, then the trade is a 1:1.2 trade — not more.
  • If logical target gives < 1:1.5 R:R: skip the trade or wait for a better entry (tighter stop) to improve the R:R ratio.
🚫
Stop Loss — The Art of Placement
▼
  • Principle: stop placement invalidates the trade thesis. If the thesis is "bullish OB + FVG in discount zone," the stop goes below the OB — the point where the thesis is wrong.
  • ATR-based minimum: stop must be at least 1.0–1.5× ATR from entry to absorb normal volatility. Tighter than 0.5× ATR = stop-hunted constantly.
  • Never move stop against yourself: the single most destructive risk habit. Moving a stop from −50 pips to −80 pips to "give it room" converts a defined loss into an open-ended disaster.
  • Moving stop to break-even: acceptable and standard practice once the trade reaches 1:1 R:R. This eliminates the risk of a winner turning into a loss.
  • Trailing stops: in strong trending moves, trail the stop below the last significant swing low (uptrend) or above the last swing high (downtrend). Use ATR or structure-based trailing, not arbitrary pip amounts.

Drawdown Management

Drawdown is not a failure — it is a structural feature of every trading strategy. The question is not whether you will have drawdowns, but whether your rules prevent drawdowns from becoming account-ending events.

The Asymmetry of Losses

A 10% loss requires an 11.1% gain to recover. A 50% loss requires 100%.

This asymmetry is the most important mathematical fact in trading. A 50% drawdown does not just hurt psychologically — it structurally requires doubling the remaining capital to break even. This is why the primary objective of risk management is capital preservation, not profit maximisation.

The Three-Level Drawdown Protocol
▼
  • Level 1 — Daily limit (3–5%): daily P&L hits the daily drawdown limit → stop trading for the day. No exceptions. The limit prevents one bad day from becoming a bad week.
  • Level 2 — Weekly limit (7–10%): weekly drawdown reaches the weekly limit → stop trading, review all trades from the week, identify what went wrong before continuing. Paper trade until confident.
  • Level 3 — Monthly limit (15–20%): monthly drawdown reaches 15% → full strategy review required. Reduce position size by 50% for the following month. Do not return to full size until 3 consecutive profitable months at reduced size.
  • Level 3 action plan: (1) catalogue every losing trade from the month, (2) categorise losses: rule violations vs. valid setups that lost, (3) fix rule violations first — these are not losses, they are discipline failures, (4) review valid losses for strategy adjustments needed.

Trade Execution

The gap between a profitable strategy on paper and a profitable strategy in practice is almost always execution. Slippage, second-guessing, premature exits and missed entries compound over hundreds of trades into a significant performance drag.

1

Plan the trade before the session begins

Set your levels, identify your entry zone, calculate your stop and target, and write the conditional: "If price does X, I enter with stop at Y and target at Z." Everything decided before markets open. Nothing decided during emotional real-time action.

2

Use limit orders, not market orders

Market orders execute at the current ask — always worse than the last printed price. Limit orders execute at your defined price or better. The difference compounds over hundreds of trades. At 50% fill rate on limit entries, the average fill quality is significantly better than market-order traders.

3

Enter once — do not average down

Averaging down (adding to a losing position) is the most common cause of account blowups. It is not a trading strategy — it is an emotional response to being wrong. Every new position must stand alone on its own entry criteria. The fact that you are losing on a previous trade is irrelevant.

4

Let the trade work — do not manage it to death

Micromanaging — watching every tick, tightening stops prematurely, closing early — is the fastest way to destroy edge. After entry, the only permissible actions are: move stop to break-even at 1:1 R:R, take partial profit at first target, let remainder run to final target or stop. Nothing else.

5

Separate results from process quality

A losing trade that followed all rules perfectly is a better trade than a winning trade that violated rules. Over 200 trades, the quality of your process determines outcomes — not individual results. Document process quality separately from P&L.

6

One trade at a time — avoid simultaneous correlated positions

Opening EUR/USD long and GBP/USD long simultaneously is not two trades — it is one USD trade with double the risk. Know your correlation exposure. At maximum, hold two uncorrelated positions simultaneously. More than that and your risk management collapses during correlated market moves.

Backtesting Your Edge

Backtesting is the systematic application of your trading rules to historical price data to measure statistical edge. Without it, you are trading hope — not a verified process.

📋
The Manual Backtest Process
▼
1
Choose your asset and timeframe.
Start with one instrument and one timeframe. EUR/USD on the 15-minute chart is a good starting point. Master one before adding others.
2
Write your rules before you look at the data.
Entry conditions, stop placement, target setting — all defined before you scroll back. Defining rules after seeing the outcome is curve-fitting, not backtesting.
3
Use a chart replay tool.
TradingView bar replay, Soft4FX, or Forex Tester. You need to go through the data candle by candle — not scroll back and "see" setups with hindsight bias.
4
Record every trade in a spreadsheet.
Date, entry, stop, target, outcome in R, notes on the setup quality. You need at minimum 100 trades before drawing any conclusions. 200 is better.
5
Out-of-sample testing.
If your strategy shows edge on Year 1 data, test it on Year 2 data (out of sample). A strategy that only works on the period you optimised it on has no real edge — it is overfitted.
⚠️
Common Backtesting Mistakes
▼
  • Hindsight bias: the biggest enemy of backtesting. When you scroll back, you already know what price did. Your brain automatically "sees" setups that confirm. Only bar replay eliminates this.
  • Overfitting: adjusting your rules until they fit the historical data perfectly. A 95% win rate on backtested data is a sign of overfitting, not edge. Real edges have realistic win rates (35–65%).
  • Cherry picking: only counting the trades that worked. Every valid setup by your rules must be counted, including the losers.
  • Ignoring spreads and slippage: add 1–2 pips of spread and 0.5–1 pip slippage to every trade. This can erode a small edge completely.
  • Insufficient sample size: 20 trades proves nothing. 50 trades proves almost nothing. 100+ trades begins to show a real picture. 300+ trades is statistically meaningful.

The Trading Plan

A trading plan is the document that governs every decision you make in the markets. It removes the need for real-time decision-making under emotional pressure — the conditions under which every trader performs worst. If you do not have a trading plan, you are not a trader. You are gambling with a chart open.

📄
The Seven Components of a Professional Trading Plan
▼
  • 1. Strategy definition: your entry criteria (exact conditions), stop placement rules, target rules. Written so specifically that someone else could trade it without asking you a single question.
  • 2. Asset universe: which instruments you trade and which you do not. For beginners: maximum 2–3 assets. Knowing your markets deeply is more valuable than being shallow on many.
  • 3. Timeframe hierarchy: which timeframe defines your HTF bias, which is your setup timeframe, which is your entry timeframe. Stated explicitly.
  • 4. Risk parameters: risk % per trade, daily drawdown limit, weekly limit, monthly limit. What you do when each limit is hit.
  • 5. Trading hours: which sessions you trade. Which hours you absolutely do not trade (news events, off-hours, Fridays after 18:00 CET etc.).
  • 6. Review schedule: when you review your journal (daily, weekly), what questions you ask during review, how you measure strategy health.
  • 7. Mental state protocol: rules for when you are NOT allowed to trade (emotional state criteria), recovery procedures after losing streaks.
