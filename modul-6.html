ICT Philosophy

ICT (Inner Circle Trader) was developed by Michael J. Huddleston. The core premise: price is not random. It is algorithmically delivered between price levels by institutional programs — and those programs leave predictable signatures that a trained eye can read.

The Core Paradigm Shift

Stop thinking about support and resistance. Start thinking about liquidity.

Classic TA sees double tops as "resistance." ICT sees accumulated stop-loss orders waiting to be hunted. Classic TA sees a trendline break as a "signal." ICT sees a liquidity delivery event. This single reframe changes every chart you will ever read.

ICT's framework rests on three principles: (1) Price seeks liquidity — it moves toward where orders are concentrated. (2) Price delivers algorithmically — patterns repeat because the same algorithms execute at the same conditions. (3) Time and price are inseparable — the best setups occur at the intersection of the right price level and the right time window (Kill Zone).

🏦

The Interbank Price Delivery Algorithm (IPDA)

ICT's theory: a global algorithm coordinates price delivery between major banks. It operates on a 20-40-60 trading day cycle, defines where price draws to next, and creates the highs and lows that retail sees as "natural" market movement.

⚡

Algorithmic Price Delivery

Price does not "search" randomly for the next level. According to ICT, it is algorithmically routed from one liquidity pool to the next. The algorithm's job: collect liquidity, create imbalance, fill it, repeat. This is why certain patterns repeat precisely.

🎯

Retail vs. Smart Money

Retail sees: trends, patterns, indicators. Smart Money sees: liquidity pools, order flow, algorithmic delivery models. The gap in perspective explains why retail consistently loses to institutional operators who exploit predictable behaviour.

⚠️

ICT is not a Holy Grail

ICT is a framework, not a mechanical system. It requires subjective identification of levels, context-reading and patience. It has a steep learning curve and is easily misapplied. A profitable ICT trader has 200–500 hours of screen time with the concepts before live trading.

02

Liquidity Theory

Liquidity is the foundation of everything in ICT. Without understanding where orders are clustered, every other concept is incomplete. Price does not move to technical levels because of "support and resistance" — it moves because that is where the orders are.

Liquidity Pool Distribution — A Typical Chart
📍
Sell-Side Liquidity (SSL) — Below Swing Lows
▼

SSL consists of stop-loss orders from long positions placed below swing lows, below round numbers and below prior support. When price sweeps these levels, it triggers the stops — creating a wave of sell orders.

  • Formation: every time retail buys at a swing low with a stop below it, they contribute to the SSL pool
  • How institutions use it: an institution wanting to buy drives price into the SSL zone, triggering stop sells, and buys into that selling pressure at a better price
  • Recognition: wicks below prior lows, especially on the daily/4H, that immediately reverse
  • Equal lows (EQL): two lows at the same price = double SSL. Price will almost certainly sweep this before reversing up
  • After sweep: look for immediate bullish order flow — this is the entry trigger in many ICT setups
📍
Buy-Side Liquidity (BSL) — Above Swing Highs
▼

BSL consists of stop-loss orders from short positions and buy stops from breakout traders placed above swing highs, above round numbers and above prior resistance.

  • Retail sees: a double top at 1.1050 → "strong resistance" → places short entry and stop above
  • ICT sees: accumulated BSL above 1.1050 → institution will run price above this level to collect all those buy stops → then sell into the buying frenzy
  • Equal highs (EQH): two highs at the same price = double BSL. A classic bull trap setup — sweep BSL, then reversal
  • Turtle soup: ICT's name for taking the opposite side of an obvious breakout. The "breakout" is the stop hunt; the trade is the reversal.
  • After sweep: look for immediate bearish order flow — candles closing well below the swept high
🔄
Liquidity Raids — The Anatomy of a Stop Hunt
▼

A liquidity raid is the deliberate price move into a liquidity pool, followed by a sharp reversal. Once you understand this, you will recognise it every single session.

  • Step 1 — Identify: where are the obvious highs, lows, equal levels? Those are the liquidity targets.
  • Step 2 — Wait for the raid: price approaches and briefly exceeds the level. Volume often spikes here.
  • Step 3 — Confirmation: a rejection candle forms above/below the level — a wick that closes back inside the range. This is the institutional sell/buy into the stop orders.
  • Step 4 — Entry: enter in the reversal direction on the close of the rejection candle or the open of the next candle.
  • Step 5 — Stop placement: above/below the wick extreme (the furthest point of the hunt).
  • Key insight: the hunt wick is the stop — not a reason to avoid the trade. The wick IS the institutional footprint.

Order Blocks

An Order Block (OB) is the last candle — or group of candles — before a strong impulsive move. This is where institutions placed their orders. When price returns to this zone (mitigation), those orders are still active and price reacts strongly.

Bullish Order Block — Structure and Mitigation
🟢
Bullish Order Block — Definition and Entry
▼
  • Definition: the last bearish (red) candle before an impulsive bullish move. The zone = the high and low of that candle (or the candle's body, depending on the ICT model version used).
  • Why it works: institutions placed buy orders inside this candle. They were not all filled during the initial move. When price returns (mitigation), the remaining orders activate.
  • Entry model: price retraces into the OB zone → forms a bullish rejection wick or engulfing → enter long at the bottom of the OB or on the wick close.
  • Stop placement: below the OB low with 1–2 ATR buffer.
  • Quality filters: (1) the impulsive move after the OB was large and fast, (2) the OB is in the direction of the HTF bias, (3) the OB is at a Fibonacci discount level (below 50%), (4) a Kill Zone is active.
  • Mitigation check: once the OB is tested once, it loses strength. Twice tested = significantly weaker. Three times = likely invalid.
🔴
Bearish Order Block — Definition and Entry
▼
  • Definition: the last bullish (green) candle before an impulsive bearish move. The zone = the high and low of that candle.
  • Entry model: price retraces into the OB zone → forms a bearish rejection → enter short at the top of the OB or on the wick close.
  • Stop placement: above the OB high with 1–2 ATR buffer.
  • Inverse OB: when a bullish OB fails (price closes through it), it can become a bearish OB — role reversal on the order block level.
  • Breaker Block: a previously mitigated OB that has been violated. The broken OB structure then acts as an opposing block. A broken bullish OB becomes a bearish Breaker Block.
🔮
Mitigation Blocks and Breaker Blocks
▼
  • Mitigation Block: an OB that was previously tested and respected, but then price continued through it on the next visit. The failed mitigation creates a market structure shift.
  • Breaker Block: a failed OB that now acts as the opposite type. A bullish OB that gets violated becomes a Bearish Breaker Block — when price returns from below, it acts as a sell zone.
  • Propulsion Block: two back-to-back OBs in the same direction. The combined zone creates a stronger support/resistance area.
  • Rejection Block: a wick that is unusually long relative to the body. The wick range acts as an OB — price will typically return to fill the wick range.
Critical Application Rule

Order blocks are most reliable when: (1) they align with a Fibonacci discount/premium zone (bullish OB below 50%, bearish OB above 50%), (2) a FVG is present within or adjacent to the OB, (3) the HTF shows a clear directional bias, and (4) a Kill Zone is active. All four present = maximum edge.

04

Fair Value Gaps

A Fair Value Gap (FVG) — also called an imbalance — forms when price moves so fast that no fair exchange could occur between buyers and sellers. The gap between two candles represents a price zone that was never efficiently traded. Markets tend to return and fill these gaps.

Fair Value Gap — Bullish and Bearish Examples
📏
Identifying FVGs — The Three-Candle Rule
▼

A FVG requires three candles:

  • Bullish FVG: the low of Candle 3 is higher than the high of Candle 1. The gap between these two levels is the FVG zone.
  • Bearish FVG: the high of Candle 3 is lower than the low of Candle 1. The gap between these levels is the FVG zone.
  • The impulse candle (middle): Candle 2 is the engine. It moved so fast that the gap between Candles 1 and 3 was never traded.
  • Quality filter: larger FVGs (bigger gap relative to ATR) are more significant. FVGs on higher timeframes (4H, Daily) are more reliable than intraday.
  • Nested FVGs: multiple FVGs in the same direction stacking on top of each other = very strong supply/demand zone.
🎯
Trading FVGs — Fill, Partial Fill and Invalidation
▼
  • Full fill: price returns to trade through the entire FVG zone. After a full fill, the FVG is "mitigated" and no longer a reliable reversal zone.
  • Partial fill: price enters the FVG and reacts at 50% of the zone (the "equilibrium" of the FVG). This is the most common and most actionable scenario.
  • Entry model: wait for price to enter the FVG zone → look for a rejection candlestick → enter with stop beyond the FVG boundary.
  • Target: the opposing liquidity pool (SSL or BSL) after mitigation of the FVG.
  • Invalidation: price closes through the entire FVG with a full-bodied candle → FVG is invalidated → do not trade it.
  • Inverted FVG: once a bullish FVG is fully closed through, it can act as resistance on the retest (now a supply zone). Same logic as OB role reversal.
🔗
FVG + OB Confluence — The Highest-Probability Zone
▼

When a FVG overlaps with an Order Block, the zone becomes one of the highest-probability reversal areas in ICT:

  • The OB shows where institutional orders were placed
  • The FVG shows that an imbalance exists that the market will seek to fill
  • Both pointing to the same price zone = maximum confluence
  • Entry precision: enter at the 50% level of the FVG that overlaps with the OB. This gives a tight stop and excellent R:R.
  • Filter: add Fibonacci discount (bullish setup below 50% of the range) for the strongest configuration possible
  • This combination — OB + FVG + Fib discount + Kill Zone timing — is the complete ICT entry model

ICT Entry Models

ICT has multiple named entry models. Each has specific conditions and a defined setup sequence. The models are not arbitrary — they are derived from algorithmic price delivery patterns that repeat at specific times and levels.

🌅
The London Open Setup (LOD)
▼

One of the highest-probability ICT setups, based on the predictable nature of the London open session (08:00–10:00 CET).

1
Define HTF Bias (Daily/4H)
Before London opens: is the daily chart bullish or bearish? This determines whether you are looking for a long or short setup.
2
Identify the Asian Range
Mark the high and low of the Asian session (00:00–08:00 CET). This range usually contains liquidity on both sides.
3
Watch the London Stop Hunt
At or just after 08:00 CET, price often raids one side of the Asian range first — hunting stops. If bullish bias: expect SSL hunt first, then the real move up.
4
Enter on the Reversal
After the stop hunt, price enters an OB or FVG. Enter in the direction of the HTF bias. Stop: beyond the hunt wick. Target: the opposing liquidity (Asian range opposite side, then beyond).
🌆
The New York Open Setup (NYO)
▼

The New York Open Kill Zone (14:30–16:00 CET) provides the second major setup window of the day.

  • Initial balance (IB): the first 60 minutes of the NY session creates the initial balance. Mark these highs and lows.
  • Setup type 1 — Continuation: London established a trend. NY open provides a pullback entry in that direction, often to a 4H OB or FVG.
  • Setup type 2 — Reversal: London move was a stop hunt (fake move in wrong direction). NY open reverses back toward the real target.
  • Confirmation signal: a displacement candle at NY open that closes decisively in one direction — often the start of the real move.
  • Key time: 14:30–15:00 CET is the highest-probability window. After 16:00 CET, probability declines.
🎭
The Judas Swing — The Classic ICT Misdirection
▼

Named after the biblical betrayal — a move that looks genuine but is actually misdirection. One of ICT's most important concepts.

  • Bullish day example: at the open (London or NY), price drops sharply below the prior low → retail shorts pile in → then price reverses violently upward → retail shorts are stopped out → the stop-out buying provides fuel for the real move up.
  • Bearish day example: price opens and initially rallies above prior high → retail longs enter the breakout → then price collapses → retail longs stop out → their stop selling accelerates the real move down.
  • Recognition: the Judas swing is fast (usually completes in 15–30 minutes at the open), takes a relatively small liquidity pool, and the reversal candles are strong displacement moves.
  • Entry: let the Judas swing complete (confirmed reversal wick), then enter in the true direction of the day. Stop: beyond the Judas swing extreme.
  • Application: trading in the direction of the Judas swing is the most common retail mistake. Trading against it (in the true direction) is the ICT edge.
📐
The Silver Bullet — Precision 15-Minute Entry
▼

A specific ICT entry model designed for the 15-minute chart during three specific time windows: 03:00–04:00 CET, 10:00–11:00 CET, 14:00–15:00 CET.

  • Conditions: HTF bias defined. Price has made a liquidity sweep (SSL or BSL) before the Silver Bullet window.
  • During the window: price displaces in the true direction, creating a 15-minute FVG.
  • Entry: enter on the 50% level of the FVG created during the Silver Bullet window.
  • Stop: beyond the FVG boundary.
  • Target: the next significant liquidity pool in the direction of the trade.
  • Why it works: these windows correspond to predictable institutional order flow events at specific New York time windows that align with IPDA algorithmic activity.

Price Delivery

ICT teaches that price is algorithmically delivered — not randomly discovered. Understanding how price delivers gives you a framework for reading the intention behind every move.

⚡
Displacement and Consolidation — The Engine of Price
▼
  • Displacement: a rapid, impulsive move in one direction — the engine candle. This is algorithmic order flow activating. Creates FVGs and BOS (Break of Structure).
  • Consolidation: the range-bound period between displacements. Liquidity builds here. The longer the consolidation, the more explosive the next displacement.
  • ICT rule: trade displacement, not consolidation. Enter after displacement creates a FVG, on the pullback into that FVG. Do not trade within the range.
  • Accumulation phases within consolidation: small, quiet OBs form during consolidation. These are accumulation zones — price will eventually displace out of them.
📊
Market Structure Shifts (MSS) and CHoCH
▼
  • Market Structure Shift (MSS): a displacement that breaks the last significant swing high (bullish MSS) or swing low (bearish MSS). Signals that the algorithm has shifted delivery direction.
  • Change of Character (CHoCH): the first break of structure against the current trend — the earliest possible signal of a trend change. Less reliable than MSS but gives an earlier entry.
  • Break of Structure (BOS): continuation signal — in an uptrend, price breaks above the last swing high = BOS bullish. In downtrend, price breaks below last swing low = BOS bearish.
  • Trading MSS: enter the first OB or FVG created after the MSS displacement, in the direction of the shift. Stop: below the MSS low (bullish) or above the MSS high (bearish).
  • Multi-timeframe application: a Daily MSS is more significant than a 15-min MSS. Higher timeframe MSS → larger position size and wider stop is appropriate.
🔄
Optimal Trade Entry (OTE) — The Fibonacci-ICT Bridge
▼
  • OTE zone: 61.8% to 79% retracement of the displacement move (the swing from the MSS origin to the displacement high/low).
  • Why: institutions accumulate into pullbacks at the OTE zone. Retail sell their longs here (thinking the move is over), institutions absorb the selling and reload.
  • Setup: displacement creates a BOS/MSS → price pulls back to 61.8–79% → OB or FVG is present within this zone → enter long/short at the zone with rejection candle.
  • Confluence stack: OTE + OB + FVG + Kill Zone + HTF bias = the highest-probability ICT setup available.
  • Target from OTE entry: minimum target = 127.2% extension of the displacement leg. Full target = 161.8%.

The Complete ICT Trade Model

Everything from this module now combines into a single repeatable decision framework. This is not a mechanical system — it is a disciplined sequence of questions answered before every trade.

1
HTF Bias — What is the daily/4H chart saying?
Is price above or below the EMA200 daily? Is structure bullish (HH+HL) or bearish (LH+LL)? Determine whether today is a buy day or sell day. No bias = no trade.
2
Liquidity Target — Where is price drawn to?
Mark the previous day/week high and low. Mark equal highs and lows. Mark recent swing highs and lows. Identify which side has more accumulated liquidity (BSL or SSL) — that is the likely target for today's move.
3
Premium / Discount — Where is price now?
Apply 50% Fibonacci to the current range. Is price in Premium (above 50%) or Discount (below 50%)? Bullish setups require discount entries. Bearish setups require premium entries. Wrong zone = skip the setup.
4
PD Array Identification — What specific level to use?
Within the correct zone, identify the highest-quality PD array: OB + FVG overlap is best. OB alone is second. FVG alone is third. Mark the exact entry zone.
5
Kill Zone Timing — Are we in the right window?
Is the London Open KZ (08:00–10:00) or NY Open KZ (14:30–16:00) active? If not, wait. The best setup in the wrong time window has significantly lower probability.
6
Liquidity Sweep Confirmation
Before entering, has a liquidity sweep occurred? SSL swept (bullish trade) or BSL swept (bearish trade)? The sweep confirms institutional activity at this level. Without a sweep: lower probability, wider stop required.
7
Entry Trigger — Confirmation Candle
Enter on the close of a rejection candle within the PD array. Bullish: wick below the OB/FVG with bullish close. Bearish: wick above the zone with bearish close. No trigger = no entry. Stop: below/above the trigger candle wick.
8
Target — The Opposing Liquidity Pool
Target 1: the liquidity that was swept (reclaim of swept level). Target 2: the next significant BSL (bullish) or SSL (bearish). Target 3: the Fibonacci extension (127.2% of the displacement leg).
The ICT Edge in One Sentence

Wait for liquidity to be taken, then enter in the true direction at a PD array, during a Kill Zone.

This is the complete ICT trade model compressed to its essence. Every step above exists to answer three questions: where is the liquidity? Where is the institutional entry zone? What time is it? When all three align — that is the trade.
